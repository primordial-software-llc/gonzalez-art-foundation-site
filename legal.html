    Notwithstanding the provisions of sections 106 and 106A, the fair use of a copyrighted work, including such use by reproduction in copies or phonorecords or by any other means specified by that section, for purposes such as criticism, comment, news reporting, teaching (including multiple copies for classroom use), scholarship, or research, is not an infringement of copyright.   • Under the Berne Convention, works of art by artists who have long been deceased lose their copyright with varying copyright lengths death depending upon the work's country of origin. In summary most works created before 1920 are in the public domain and are no longer under copyright.

  • According to Bridgeman Art Library v. Corel Corp., unoriginal pictures of works of art in the public domain can't be copyrighted.
